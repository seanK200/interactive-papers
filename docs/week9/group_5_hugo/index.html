Victor Hugo, “Speech on Africa” at a banquet commemorating the abolition of slavery (May 18, 1879)

It has been a joy for me to be able to shake the hands of so many distinguished men who have left a good memory in the memorable human liberation that we celebrate today.

Sirs, the current moment will be remembered in this century. It’s a point of arrival, it’s a point of departure. It has its physiognomy: in the north, despotism, in the south, liberty; in the north, the storm, in the south, peace. As for ourselves, since we are simply seekers of truth, since we are dreamers, writers, attentive philosophers; since we are gathered here around a single idea, the improvement of the human race; since we are, in one word, passionate men occupied with this great subject—man—let us take advantage of this meeting, let us fix our eyes on the future; let us ask ourselves what the twentieth century will bring.

Politically, you feel it, I do not need to tell you. Geographically,-- allow me to limit myself with this designation—the destiny of men is in the south.

The moment has come to give the old world this warning: you must become a new world. The moment has come to make Europe notice that she has Africa at her side. The moment has come to tell the four nations from which modern history emerged—Greece, Italy, Spain, France—that they are still there, that their mission has been modified without being transformed, that they are still in the same position of sovereign authority on the shores of the Mediterranean, and that, if we add to them a fifth people, those who were interpolated by Virgil and who showed themselves worthy of his regard, England, we have, nearly, all the effort of the human race of antiquity towards work, which is progress, and towards unity, which is life.

The Mediterranean is a lake of civilization; it is certainly not for nothing that the Mediterranean has on its shores the old universe and the other ignored universe, that is to say, on one side all civilization and on the other all barbarism.

The moment has come to say to this illustrious group of nations: Unite! Go southward.

Do you not see the barrier? It is there, in front of you, this block of sand and cinders, this inert and passive hodgepodge, that, for six thousand years, has been an obstacle for the universal march, this monstrous Cham that stops Sem with its enormity—Africa.

What a land is Africa! Asia has its history, America has its history, even Australia has its history; Africa has no history. A sort of vast and obscure legend envelopes it. Rome touched it, in order to suppress it; and, when it believed itself to be delivered from Africa, Rome threw on this immense death, one of these epithets that cannot be translated: Africa portentosa! (Applause) It’s more and less than the feat. It is what is absolute in the horror. The tropical flaming, in effect, it is Africa. It seems that to see Africa, one must be blind. An excess of sun is an excess of night.

Indeed, this fear must disappear.

Already the two colonizing peoples, who are the two great free peoples, France and England, have seized Africa; France holds it by the west and by the north; England holds it by the east and the center. Here Italy accepts its part of this colossal work. America joins its efforts to ours; because the unity of peoples is revealed in everything. Africa matters to the universe. Such a suppression of the movement and circulation hinders universal life, and the human march cannot accommodate itself any longer to a fifth of the globe being paralyzed.

Sturdy pioneers have risked it, and, from their first steps, this strange soil has appeared real; these lunar landscapes have become terrestrial landscapes. France is ready to bring a sea there. This wild Africa has only two faces: populated, it is barbarism; deserted, it is savagery; but it no longer is evasive; the places reputed to be inhabitable are possible climates; one finds everywhere navigable rivers; forests rise, vast branches encumber here and there, the horizon; what will be the attitude of civilization in front of this unknown flora and fauna? Lakes have appeared, who knows? Maybe [it is] this sea Nagain of which the Bible speaks. Gigantic hydraulic apparatuses are prepared by nature and await man; one sees the spots where cities are born; one images the communications; mountain ranges are drawn; peaks, passes, straights are passable: this universe that frightened the Romans, attracts the French.

Notice with what majesty great things are accomplished. Obstacles exist, as I have already said, they do their duty, which is to be conquered. This is not without difficulty. In the north, I insisted, a movement is being undertaken the divide ut regnes executes a colossal effort, the supreme monarchical powers create it. The German empire unites with all of its power against all that it supposes to be the modern spirit; the Muscovite empire offers an even more moving picture. To authority without limit, resists something that also has no limit; the omnipotent despotism that a million men give to one individual who cries: I want everything, I take everything! I have everything!—the abyss makes this terrible response: Nihil. And today we assist in the terrifying struggle between Nothing with Everything. (Enthusiastic applause) Spectacle worthy of meditation! Nothing engenders chaos. The social question has never been posed in such a tragic fashion, but rage is not a solution. Also, we hope that the vast breath of the 19th century will be felt as far as these distant regions, and will substitute bellicose convulsions with pacifist resolutions. However, if the north is worrisome, the center is reassuring. To the south, a close tie grows and strengthens between France, Italy and Spain. At heart, we are essentially the same people, and Greece is added to that, because the Latin origins suppose a Greek origin. These nations have the Mediterranean, and England greatly needs the Mediterranean to separate themselves from the four people who are its masters. Already the United States of the South sketch a draft of the United States of Europe. (Bravos) No hatred, no violence, no anger. This is the great peaceful march towards harmony, fraternity and peace.

To the popular acts should be added the acts of humans; the definitive form can be seen; the gigantic group can be divined; and, in order not to pass over the limits that you have set out for yourselves, in order to stay with in the order of things where it is agreed that I will remain, I limit myself, and this will be my final word, to note this detail, which is only a detail, but which is immense: in the nineteenth century, the white has made of the black a man; in the twentieth century, Europe will make of Africa a world. (Applause.)

Refashion a new Africa, remake old Africa ready for civilization, such is the problem. Europe will resolve it.

Go, peoples! Seize this land.Take it! For whom? For nobody. Take this land for God. God gave the earth to men, God offered Africa to Europe. Take it. Where the kings would bring war, bring peace. Take it, not for the cannon, but for the plough; not for the sword, but for trade; not for battles, but for industry; not for conquest, but for fraternity. (Prolonged applause.)

Pour your excess energy in this Africa, and at the same time resolve your social questions, change your proletariat into property-owners. Go, do it! Make roads, make ports, make cities; grow, cultivate, colonize, multiply; and that, on this earth, [which is] more and more disengaged from priests and from princes, the divine Spirit asserts itself with peace and the human Spirit with liberty!

(This speech, constantly interrupted with enthusiastic applause, was followed by an explosion of shouts of “Long live Hugo! Long live the Republic!”)
