Ethnology [1]

South-Western Ethiopia the country that lies south of the river Abay and west of the head-waters of the Webi-Shabelle, was occupied before the Galla invasion by peoples of Hamitic [2] stock who may be referred to as Sidama and represent one of the waves of Europoid immigrants known as Hamites are Kushites. The first inhabitants of north-east Africa seemed to have been negroes, who were gradually expelled or absorbed by the Hamitic populations that followed them. That there was contact between the two races is clear from the negroid physical characters of many of the inhabitants of Ethiopia; and in fact there still remain a number of the tribes of the Abyssinian-Sudan border which may be taken to represent the original negro population and are known collectively as Shangalla, a name derived from the Amharic word for ‘negro’. These negro peoples include some of the tribes of Dar Fung, and others extending as far south as the Konso between Lake Chamo an Lake Stephanie.

The Hamites seemed to have entered north-east Africa in 3 main waves which may be distinguished thus: (A) the Proto-or A-Hamites, perhaps represented today by the Nera or ‘Barya’ and the Badena or Kunama of Eritrea, though both the ethnic and the linguistic position of these people is very uncertain. (B) The early or B-Hamites, represented by the peoples of the northern zone (Beja), central zone (Agau), and southwestern zone (Sidama). (C) The Later of C-Hamites, represented by the peoples of the eastern zone (Galla, Somali, Afar or Danakil, and Saho). During the later periods of the Hamitic movement there came yet another wave of immigration this time of Semitic peoples from south-west Arabia, the ancestors of the present day Semitic speaking Abyssinians.
